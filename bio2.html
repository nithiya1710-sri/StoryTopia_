Chapter 1: The Beginning

Harriet Tubman, born Araminta Ross around 1822 on a Maryland plantation, grew up in the brutal reality of slavery. From a young age, she endured harsh labor and severe punishments, but even as a child, she was known for her strong will and determination. Harriet's deep sense of injustice was solidified when, at the age of 12, she was struck on the head by a heavy metal weight, causing lifelong seizures and visions. These visions, which she believed were messages from God, would guide her throughout her life. In 1849, at around 27 years old, Harriet made the brave decision to escape to freedom. Chapter 3: The Conclusion

ver the next decade, Harriet Tubman became a legendary figure in the Underground Railroad, a secret network of safe houses and abolitionists dedicated to helping enslaved people reach freedom. Using her knowledge of the landscape and her faith, Harriet led approximately 13 missions back into slave territory, rescuing more than 70 enslaved men, women, and children, including several of her family members. She never lost a single person on her missions, earning the nickname "Moses" for leading her people to freedom. Tubman’s success was due not only to her courage but also her careful planning. She always carried a revolver, both for protection and to motivate anyone who considered turning back.
